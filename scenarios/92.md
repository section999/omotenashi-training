## Turn 1

**System:** A guest approaches the concierge desk and asks for a Japanese restaurant recommendation nearby. You need to ask about their preferences before recommending.

**Guest:** We'd love to try a really good Japanese restaurant tonight — somewhere nearby. Any recommendations?
**Guest JP:** 今夜、近くで美味しい日本料理のお店に行きたいのですが、どこかおすすめはありますか？

### Choices

- [2] 「ありがとうございます。お好みをお伺いしてもよろしいでしょうか？たとえばお鮨のカウンタースタイルや懐石料理、あるいは気軽な居酒屋風など、ご希望はございますか？」
  - EN: Thank you for asking. May I ask about your preferences? For example, do you have a preference for sushi counter-style dining, kaiseki cuisine, or perhaps a more relaxed izakaya atmosphere?
  - Feedback: Ideal — before recommending, gathering the guest's preferences shows genuine attentiveness and ensures the recommendation will truly match their expectations, a hallmark of concierge excellence.
- [1] 「近くに人気の和食レストランがいくつかございます。お鮨はいかがでしょうか？」
  - EN: There are several popular Japanese restaurants nearby. How about sushi?
  - Feedback: Offering sushi is reasonable, but jumping to a specific suggestion without first asking about preferences risks missing the mark. It's acceptable but shows less finesse than gathering information first.
- [0] 「ホテルのレストランでも日本料理をご提供しております。いかがでしょうか？」
  - EN: Our hotel restaurant also serves Japanese cuisine. How about that?
  - Feedback: While promoting the hotel restaurant can be appropriate, doing so when a guest specifically asks for a place nearby comes across as self-serving rather than helpful. It fails to address the guest's actual request.
- [0] 「日本料理はたくさんありますので、インターネットで検索されてみてはいかがでしょうか？」
  - EN: There are many Japanese restaurants, so you might want to try searching the internet.
  - Feedback: Directing a guest to search online themselves is a complete failure of the concierge role. Guests expect personal, expert guidance — this response is dismissive and unhelpful.

## Turn 2

**System:** The guest confirms they would love a sushi counter experience — something authentic and intimate, not a chain restaurant. Staff now recommends a specific place with relevant details.

**Guest:** Oh yes, a sushi counter would be wonderful — somewhere authentic, not a chain.
**Guest JP:** そうですね、カウンターでいただけるお鮨屋さんがいいです。チェーン店じゃなくて、本格的なところを希望します。

### Choices

- [2] 「かしこまりました。ホテルから徒歩約5分のところに『鮨 匠 はせ川』という大将が一人で握る本格的なカウンター鮨の名店がございます。地元でも評判が高く、旬の食材を使ったおまかせコースが人気でございます。本日のご予約もコンシェルジュよりお取りすることができますが、いかがでしょうか？」
  - EN: Certainly. About a 5-minute walk from the hotel, there is an excellent sushi counter restaurant called Sushi Sho Hasegawa, where the master chef prepares everything personally. It is highly regarded locally, and their seasonal omakase course is very popular. We can also make a reservation for you from the concierge — shall I do that?
  - Feedback: This is the ideal response. A specific named restaurant with distance, a distinctive feature (solo master chef), and cuisine style is given, followed by a seamless offer to make the reservation — covering all the guest's needs in one confident reply.
- [1] 「ホテルの近くにカウンター鮨のお店がございます。ご予約はご自身でされますか？お電話番号をお伝えします。」
  - EN: There is a sushi counter restaurant near the hotel. Will you make the reservation yourself? I can give you the phone number.
  - Feedback: Providing a phone number is acceptable, but asking the guest to make their own reservation defeats the purpose of having a concierge. The recommendation is also vague — no name, distance, or distinguishing details are given.
- [0] 「お鮨でしたら、駅前に回転寿司がございます。手頃でおすすめです。」
  - EN: For sushi, there is a conveyor belt sushi restaurant near the station. It's affordable and I recommend it.
  - Feedback: The guest explicitly asked for an authentic, non-chain experience. Recommending a kaiten (conveyor belt) sushi restaurant completely ignores the guest's stated preference and shows poor attentiveness.
- [0] 「本格的な鮨屋は予約が取りにくいので、難しいかもしれません。」
  - EN: Authentic sushi restaurants are hard to get reservations at, so it might be difficult.
  - Feedback: Leading with obstacles before even attempting to help is defeatist and uninspiring. A skilled concierge proactively finds solutions — expressing doubt before trying is poor hospitality.

## Turn 3

**System:** The guest is delighted with the recommendation and asks the concierge to book a table for two at 7 PM that evening.

**Guest:** That sounds perfect! Could you book a table for two at 7 o'clock tonight?
**Guest JP:** 素晴らしいですね！ぜひ今夜7時に2名で予約をお願いできますか？

### Choices

- [2] 「かしこまりました。本日7時にお二人様でご予約を承ります。お客様のお名前と、ご連絡先のお電話番号をお伺いできますでしょうか？予約が確定しましたら、すぐにお部屋へご連絡いたします。」
  - EN: Certainly. I will arrange a reservation for two at 7 PM tonight. May I have your name and a contact phone number? Once the reservation is confirmed, I will contact you in your room right away.
  - Feedback: Ideal — the concierge takes charge of all the details, asks for what the restaurant will need (name and contact), and commits to follow-up communication in the room. This gives the guest complete peace of mind.
- [1] 「はい、予約してみます。うまくいったらお部屋にご連絡します。」
  - EN: Yes, I will try to make a reservation. If it works out, I will contact your room.
  - Feedback: The tentative language 「してみます」 (will try) and 「うまくいったら」 (if it works out) creates unnecessary uncertainty. A concierge should project confidence and provide a clear process even if the outcome requires a follow-up call.
- [0] 「予約はお客様から直接お電話いただいた方がスムーズです。番号はこちらです。」
  - EN: It would be smoother if you call the restaurant directly. Here is the number.
  - Feedback: After the guest has explicitly asked the concierge to make the booking, refusing to do so and pushing the task back to the guest is a significant failure of service. This is the concierge's core function.
- [0] 「7時ですと人気の時間帯ですので、お断りされる可能性もございます。他のお時間はいかがでしょうか？」
  - EN: 7 PM is a popular time, so there is a possibility they may decline. Would another time work for you?
  - Feedback: While it may be true that 7 PM is busy, discouraging the guest before even attempting the call is poor practice. A skilled concierge should try first and only offer alternatives if truly necessary.
